Legal Claims:

Common Law Larceny. Under the Guiding Principles of Civil Dispute, absent "clear and compelling testimony or documentation to the contrary, the person in possession of the property is presumed to be the rightful owner". Additionally, the Guiding Principles of Azalea Law permit compensatory damages to be "awarded by a court equivalent to the loss a party suffered. The amount awarded is based on the proven harm, loss, or injury suffered by the plaintiff" (see also (2) below) and for punitive damages to be awareded "when it is determined that the defendant has acted in a particularly harmful way abnormal of typical situations". Special damages are also authorized when it is "proven that these damages came from the initial action and did result in actual harm".

From these principles, it arises that (1) the law recognizes rightful owner of property and (2) that Courts may provide relief to this effect.

In order to protect the guiding principles on right to property and assess damages, the Plaintiff asks the Court to recognize a common law tort of larceny in line with the Guiding Principles. This tort would consist of the taking of another's property without authorization under the law, and with intent to permanently deprive the rightful owner of that property. Tortfeasors would be subject to payment of damages, in line with the Guiding Principles.

Applying the common law tort to the facts here, the Defendant took the Plaintiff's financial property without legal authorization (indeed, this conduct is prohibited under Criminal Code Foundation Act Section 5(2)), and did not return funds despite it being trivially easy to do so.
Compensatory Damages. Under the Guiding Principles of Azalea Law, compensatory damages are "awarded by a court equivalent to the loss a party suffered. The amount awarded is based on the proven harm, loss, or injury suffered by the plaintiff". As demonstrated in the third factual allegation (as supported by Exhibit P-001), Defendant stole $76.88 from the Plaintiff, a concrete loss suffered by the Plaintiff.
Punitive Damages. Under the Guiding Principles, punitive damages are authorized in circumstances in which a "defendant has acted in a particularly harmful way abnormal of typical situations". Defendant killed the Plaintiff (see: Factual Allegation No. 1; Exhibit P-001) and robbed the Plaintiff (see: Factual Allegations No. 2-4; Exhibit P-001), thus acting in a particularly harmful way that it outside of the normal course of life here in our peaceful isles.
Legal Fees. Under the Damages Reform Act, "[t]he judiciary of the Azalea Isles shall recognize a minimum legal cost of $1,000 per week incurred by parties represented by a lawyer in a trial". Plaintiff Multi Man is a lawyer, and is proceeding pro se. Thus, as a lawyer representing himself in a trial, the Plaintiff is indeed represented by a lawyer, and we thus ask the Court to award legal fees.

The parliamentary intent recorded in the Damages Civil Suit Reform Act, as is relevant to this claim, is that the law "aims to set a minimum, recognized floor for legal damages". The compensation would be for the time it took Plaintiff to file this case as a lawyer for Plaintiff's self (see: Exhibit P-002 and Exhibit P-003). In order to file this case, Plaintiff had to do legal research, gather exhibits, and draft and file the actual complaint. Plaintiff fully acknowledges that under Par. 2(a)(i) of the Damages Reform Act that awards may be diminished ("[t]he presiding judge or justice may revise this value downward for any specific week of a trial if not much was achieved that week: IE, only one statement was submitted"). Plaintiff also acknowledge that this set of three filings occurred in less than one week, but does ask that the Court recognize the cost to Plaintiff's time and pro-rate the filing. As the case was filed on Sunday, and it is now Friday, 5/7 of one week would correspond to $714 at a per-day pro-rated rate for which Plaintiff presently pleads.
This is indeed a civil trial, and Plaintiff's rationale for asking for punitive damages proceeds from the Court's ruling in White Shadow & Benjamin Higgins v. Skibidi Fart (2025) CV 06. In that case, the Plaintiff there sought punitive damages for larceny (as the Plaintiff does here) and extortion (see: Plaintiff's Claim for Relief No. 2 "Punitive damages in the amount of $2,000, to punish the Defendant's apparent larceny and extortion"). The trial Court reduced the claim from the full amount sought due to a criminal conviction on robbery and trespassing, but did not eliminate it. The citation of the Criminal Code Foundation Act is intended to be a persuasive reference as to the amount of punitive damages warranted, but the claim itself proceeds from the Guiding Principles (see Complaint, Legal Claim No. 3).
Yes. Under a recent ruling by this Court in Iturgen Bolir v. Gaven Carnahan (2026) CV 01, there is firm precedent for compensatory damages to be awarded for victims of robbery. In this case, the Defendant's stealing of funds is properly alleged in the third factual allegation and supported by Exhibit P-001, so the first and second claim for relief are strengthened by precedent.

1. The Defendant, Carrot Guy (ThatCarrotGuy1), is hereby ordered to provide $76.88 in compensatory damages to the Plaintiff.
2. The Defendant, Carrot Guy (ThatCarrotGuy1), is hereby ordered to provide $50 in punitive damages to the Plaintiff.
3. The Defendant, Carrot Guy (ThatCarrotGuy1), is hereby ordered to provide $142.85 in legal fees to the Plaintiff.

In the event that the Defendant is unable to pay these funds to the Plaintiff themselves, the Plaintiff may use this ruling to ask that the government facilitate such transaction.

Be advised that a default judgement is entered based on only the facts presented in the case filing given the Defendant's inability to respond well beyond the specified deadlines. The Court is hereby adjourned.
