Legal Claims:

I. The Speaker violated the Parliamentary Procedure Act and the Constitution when handling floor and debate proceedings on the Real Crime Crackdown Act.

I.1 General Legal Background: Parliamentary Procedure and Constitution.

The Constitution tasks the Parliament “with the authority to make laws, levy taxes, and oversee the functioning of the government” (Const., Article 2). As one of the three co-equal branches, the Parliament has “the quasi ability to fulfil obligations necessary for the performance of laws, given those laws are within the bounds of the constitution, and in nature constitutional” (Const., Article 2).

To function orderly and according to clear rules, the Parliament adopts legislative procedure. At times this is done through law, such as the Parliamentary Procedure Act (hereafter, the PPA). At other times, this has been done through Standing Orders (c.f. Continuity Act, Section 2(f); PPA, Section 9(b)(3); Orderly Parliament Act, Repeal No. 2).

The District Court has long- acknowledged “right of the Parliament to set its own procedures” (Crumplesnatch v. Azalea Isles (2025) CV 02). To do this, the Parliament has enshrined several key procedures into law through the Parliamentary Procedure Act, particularly so after the amendments from the Orderly Parliament Act. This includes, among other items, the process for Proposing Bills (PPA, Section 5), engaging in Parliamentary Debate (PPA, Section 7), and procedures for voting (PPA, Section 8).

I.2 Further Background: Responsibilities and Duties of the Speaker.

To enforce these parliamentary rules, the Constitution vests power in a Speaker of the Parliament (Const., Article 3), whose election is subject both to law and “legislative procedure” and whose responsibilities are “maintaining decorum within the chamber and managing voting protocols”. The Parliament has further specified additional powers of the Speaker in statute, and imposes three duties upon the Speaker within Section 9(b) of the PPA:

(i) Conducting and facilitating the parliamentary business including voting
(ii) Maintaining order and fairness in debates and the floor.
(iii) Enforcing the Parliamentary Procedure Act, Standing Orders, and general ethical standards in an impartial manner.

For this claim, the Plaintiff alleges that the Speaker acted against procedure (and, thus, ultra vires) with regards to the handling of the proposed Real Crime Crackdown Act. Now that we know the issue, we should proceed to understanding what the relevant rule is: what are the current procedures, and how does that relate to this case?

I.3 Parliamentary rules, as established in the PPA, require the Speaker to be notified by a bill’s introducing MP prior to opening debate on that bill.

Every member of Parliament is empowered to introduce their own legislation (PPA, Section 5(a)). This may be done by placing a bill draft onto the Bill Proposals forum (see, for example, Exhibit P-004). Thereafter, “[o]nce this has been done the bill is proposed and they can notify the speaker to open debate” (PPA, Section 5(b), emphasis mine). After receiving this notification, the Speaker may open debate (PPA, Section 7(a), “[a]ny bill that is introduced will be considered by the house as soon as the speaker opens the debate. Once the debate has opened members will be notified by the speaker”). Debate continues until either a closure motion is passed (PPA, Section 7(b,d)) or voting begins following 48 hours of debate (PPA, Section 7(c)).

In this case, the Plaintiff alleges that the Speaker placed the Real Crime Crackdown Act, which the Plaintiff introduced, up for debate without receiving notification from the Plaintiff to do so (Factual Allegations 2, 5, 6, 16). Upon being challenged, the Speaker maintained that the Speaker put it up on his own accord and did not need to notify themself to put the bill up for debate (Factual Allegations 10, 11; Exhibit P-002).

So where does the disagreement that is at the locus of this dispute come from. First, the Speaker erred in assessing who “they” in PPA Section 5(b) refers to. As must interpret each clause in a bill as having a real effect (see: Luke Thegreatfired v. Lysander Lyon (2026) CV 09, Order of Dismissal, “Considering the principle of the anti-surplusage canon, statutes must be interpreted so that every provision has effect”), and in the greater structural context of the law, the answer is clear: the use of “they” in PPA Section 5(b) is a singular they that refers to the same “member” who acted to introduce a bill under PPA Section 5(a). The member who introduces a bill notifies the Speaker when the bill is ready for debate, and the Speaker thereafter will open debate.

The Speaker disagreed, bolding “they can” in the text of PPA Section 5(b) in responding to the Plaintiff and implying that the clause is read in a broadly permissive way that gives the Speaker ultimate discretion over when to start debates of his own accord (Exhibit P-003). But even permissive phrasing can create a sponsor-controlled trigger; reading 5(b) as non-limiting risks voiding the function of the sponsor-notification clause entirely. The District Court has expressly applied anti-surplusage reasoning to avoid interpretations that nullify statutory text (see: Luke Thegreatfired v. Lysander Lyon (2026) CV 09, Order of Dismissal); the Speaker’s reading would do nothing short of de facto nullifying the statutory handshake between the MP and the Speaker. The Speaker's reading, thus, is plain error. Such attempts to relegate statutory text to surplus hold no water in the District Court.

I.4 By starting debate without notification from the Plaintiff, the Speaker acted ultra vires in a way that materially impacted the Plaintiff under both statute and the constitution.

The Plaintiff is a Member of Parliament who wrote and introduced the Real Crime Crackdown Act. Under PPA Section 5(b), the Speaker should not have put the bill up for debate prior to receiving a notification to do so. By doing so anyway, the Speaker short-circuited the Plaintiff in the wee hours of the morning, depriving the Plaintiff of the ability to speak with other MPs and lobby on behalf of his bill.

Why did the Speaker do this? The Speaker also had his own bill on the floor at the time that addressed a similar subject matter (Exhibit P-005). The Speaker introduced it about 2 hours before the Plaintiff introduced the Real Crime Crackdown Act. What were the Speaker’s thoughts on the Plaintiff’s bill? The thoughts were simple: he said “This bill stinks” (Exhibit P-001) and then motioned to close debate a mere 6 minutes after opening it unilaterally. The Speaker’s motive to shut down the bill is quite clear.

This is a clear-cut violation of the Speaker’s duty to “maintain[]... fairness in debates and the floor” (PPA Section 9(b)(2)) and to “[e]nforc[e] the Parliamentary Procedure Act… in an impartial manner” (PPA Section 9(b)(3)). The Speaker must not be allowed to bypass statutory rules that a MP to provide notice to the Speaker when their bill is ready for debate, ignore furious objections from that MP who protested that he had not notified the Speaker that the bill was ready for debate, and then almost immediately move to shut down the debate because of dislike of that bill. MPs, like the Plaintiff, are entitled to basic fairness and impartiality from the Speaker in administering the rules; failure to do so causes a violation of statutory rights, especially when ultra vires actions were the immediate cause of the unfairness.

To make matters worse, the Constitution provides citizens “[t]he right against government overreach of powers not specified by the constitution” (Const. Article 1, Section 10). If the speaker were to have overreached here by violating statute-enshrined procedure in a manner that were to harm an MP’s ability to execute their job, it would cause constitutional injury (as do all such ultra vires actions).

I.5 The District Court has authority to oversee legal disputes arising from a Speaker’s failure to faithfully adhere to parliamentary procedure.

In Crumplesnatch v. Azalea Isles (2025) CV 02, the defense raised a separation of powers objection (i.e., that standing orders/parliamentary procedure are internal and not reviewable). The court nevertheless indicated it could determine whether parliamentary procedures were complied with, addressing Speaker obligations under the Parliamentary Procedure Act and providing concrete remedial direction (including directing Parliament to clarify procedure and discounting an improper deciding vote).

And in Crumplesnatch v. Bezzer (2024) CV 02, the court entered a default judgment that included the removal of the Speaker based on unlawful voting—demonstrating that the judiciary, at least in some procedural contexts, will impose direct consequences for procedural illegality tied to Speakership conduct.

I.6 Conclusion 1: The Speaker unlawfully acted outside of his authority in unilaterally opening debate on the Real Crime Crackdown Act.

Section 5 of the PPA contemplates a sponsor-controlled trigger to open debate; that is, notification of the speaker. Defendant’s opening of debate absent Plaintiff’s notification/request exceeded Defendant’s authority under the Act and is unlawful.

The Parliamentary Procedure Act (PPA) provides that once a bill is proposed, “they can notify the speaker to open debate.” Plaintiff alleges “they” refers to the bill’s proposer/sponsor, and that the Speaker’s unilateral opening—absent Plaintiff’s notice—was not authorized by statute and therefore ultra vires.

This court has previously “acknowledge[d] the importance of maintaining procedural continuity to ensure fairness and stability in the legislative process” (Crumplesnatch v. Azalea Isles (2025) CV 02, Court Opinion, Section 1), as well as the “the critical importance of maintaining public confidence in the integrity of parliamentary procedures” (ibid, Section 2). By failing to adhere to parliamentary procedures, the Speaker has undermined confidence in the integrity of parliamentary procedure, deprived the Plaintiff of the ability to begin debate when the Plaintiff was ready to do so, and violated the statutory scheme that the Parliamentary Procedure Act has laid out. In acting ultra vires, the Speaker also violated the Plaintiff’s right against government overreach.

In short, the Speaker acted outside of his lawfully granted authority, and in doing so materially impacted the Plaintiff as an MP. This injury is cognizable and deserves relief.

I.7 Conclusion 2: The Speaker unlawfully failed to maintain fairness and impartiality when handling the Real Crime Crackdown Act.

The PPA imposes duties on the Speaker to conduct parliamentary business and maintain order and fairness (PPA Section 9(b)(2-3)). Opening debate without ‘s prior notice and then more or less immediately moving to end debate—after publicly disparaging the bill and having proposed a different bill on a similar subject matter—breaches those duties and demonstrates partial, outcome-driven administration. This creates a cognizable injury that warrants relief.

II. Nominal Damages

Under the Guiding Principles of Civil Dispute, nominal damages are “awarded to a plaintiff in instances where the statutory law or common law can provide no other monetary equity or award for damages”. In this case, Plaintiff’s statutory and constitutional rights were violated (see generally: Legal Claims, Section I), and thus Plaintiff is entitled to an award of nominal damages to recognize such violations.

III. Legal Fees

Under the Damages Reform Act that "[t]he judiciary of the Azalea Isles shall recognize a minimum legal cost of $1,000 per week incurred by parties represented by a lawyer in a trial". Plaintiff is represented by counsel in this case and intends to seek legal fees.

Your Honour,

The State respectfully opposes this emergency injunction for the reasons listed below.

(1) Plaintiff’s claims are unlikely to succeed on the merits.

Section 5(b) of the Parliamentary Procedure Act provides that once a bill has been proposed, Members of Parliament “can notify the speaker to open debate.” Under the Plain Meaning Rule of statutory interpretation, the word “can” denotes permission, not obligation. The statute allows a member to notify the Speaker but does not impose any binding requirement that the Speaker must await such notification before opening debate.

Plaintiff’s reliance on Thegreatfired v. Lyon is misplaced. That case addressed a materially different issue and does not transform permissive statutory language into a mandatory condition. The Plaintiff’s theory of a “statutory handshake” ignores the optional wording chosen by the legislature.

Moreover, the Parliamentary Procedure Act expressly assigns to the Speaker the authority to interpret and enforce.

Section 9(b)(iii) charges the Speaker with “enforcing the Parliamentary Procedure Act, Standing Orders, and general ethical standards in an impartial manner.” This responsibility necessarily includes reasonable interpretation and administration of parliamentary procedure.

Section 7(a) provides further clarity:

“Any bill that is introduced will be considered by the house as soon as the speaker opens the debate.”

The statute therefore places responsibility for opening debate with the Speaker. The law makes clear that managing the timing and initiation of debate falls within the Speaker’s procedural authority, including the reasonable interpretation and enforcement of the Act.

Accordingly, Plaintiff has not demonstrated unlawful or ultra vires conduct and is unlikely to succeed on the merits.

(2) There is no imminent irreparable harm justifying an emergency injunction.

Emergency relief should not be granted lightly. The plaintiff should show immediate and irreparable harm. Neither exists here.

Plaintiff remains free to reintroduce the bill through ordinary parliamentary processes should it fail. Plaintiff suffers absolutely no harm if this bill were to move to the voting stage.

Because no permanent or irreversible injury has occurred, an emergency injunction is unwarranted.

(3)The balance of equities clearly favours Parliament.

Granting the injunction would require judicial interference in ongoing parliamentary procedure and risk placing routine legislative administration under court supervision.

Courts have previously recognised Parliament’s authority to manage its own internal processes, and denying Parliament's right to control its own docket weighs strongly against such intervention.

The Parliamentary Procedure Act already provides internal remedies where a member believes the Speaker has acted partially or in error. Section 9(d) states, "If a member believes the speaker has made an error or acted in a partial way they may choose to override a decision made by the speaker with a simple majority motion requiring the support of at least two other members to be proposed."

Denying the injunction preserves the status quo while allowing Parliament to continue functioning, and this case to proceed in the ordinary course.

In conclusion, because the plaintiff has failed to demonstrate a likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, or equities favouring relief, the remedy of an emergency injunction should therefore be denied.

Motion to Dismiss

Your Honour, we request dismissal. This is a dispute for Parliament to resolve, via procedures outlined in statutory law, and the law is clear that the Speaker is responsible for enforcement of the Act.

Parliamentary procedure, as outlined in statutory law, establishes a clear way to resolve procedural disputes. Section 9(d) of the Parliamentary Procedure Act allows Members of Parliament to overturn decisions and interpretations made by the Speaker of Parliament via motion. The legislature has established this path to resolve claims like the ones raised in this case, and yet Plaintiff has not made such a motion in Parliament to overturn the actions he is suing over. Exercising judicial review in a procedural dispute where Parliament has explicitly set up a method for contesting procedural interpretations would be a significant violation of separation of powers. This dispute should follow the proper, legally-defined channels for contesting a decision, which allow Parliament to manage the interpretation of its own procedure already set in law.

While the Plaintiff references Crumplesnatch v. Azalea, there are clear differences between these cases. The court established the power of review due to the possibility of the Speaker failing to enforce his duties under the law. The Speaker is enforcing the law in this case, and has done so uniformly; Plaintiff's disagreement with the interpretation of this enforcement already enshrined in the law is subject to the resolution mechanism of 9(d), not judicial review.

Furthermore, the law is clear that the Speaker has the power to manage the opening of debate. Section 5(b) reads, "Once this has been done the bill is proposed and [the member] can notify the speaker to open debate." The wording here is optional, and as the Plaintiff has already acknowledged, permissive. Bills are introduced by members, per Section 5(a), and then considered proposed, per 5(b), and ready to be brought to debate. Members notify the Speaker of this status. Section 7(a) reinforces this interpretation: "Any bill that is introduced will be considered by the house as soon as the speaker opens the debate. Once the debate has opened members will be notified by the speaker."

Any introduced bill may be considered by the legislature, only once the Speaker opens debate. Plaintiff's claims of a "statutory handshake" are reflected nowhere in the legislation. 5(a) asserts MPs' rights to introduce legislation, 5(b) ensures all introduced legislation is considered proposed and ready for debate, and 7(a) authorizes the Speaker to move that legislation into debate.

Therefore, we respectfully request dismissal.

Motion to Strike
Your Honor, the Defense motions to strike Factual Allegations 22-24. The Plaintiff cannot claim to know the state of mind of the Speaker, and Allegations 23 and 24 are legal claims, not facts.

Answer to Civil Complaint:

The Speaker executed a proper interpretation of Parliamentary procedure, as outlined in statutory law. That same law outlines the proper, legal channel for redress, which the Plaintiff does not seem to have attempted, instead asking the court to establish further jurisdiction over the legislative branch, a clear violation of the separation of powers.

Parties:
Plaintiff - Anthony_Org
Defendant - Azalea Isles

Factual Defenses:
(1) Pursuant to Section 9(b)iii, the Speaker is responsible for enforcing the Parliamentary Procedure Act.

(2) The dispute in this case is not over a lack of enforcement, but over an interpretation of the Parliamentary Procedure Act and the enforcement of that interpretation.

(3) The Parliamentary Procedure Act makes no explicit binding requirement on the Speaker's ability to bring bills to debate, aside from them being introduced to Parliament.

Legal Defenses
Plaintiff primarily draws his arguments from the claim that the Speaker may not open debate on a bill unless the bill’s sponsor first notifies the Speaker; from Thegreatfired v. Lyon; and from Crumplesnatch v. Azalea Isles.

(1) Plaintiff's primary claim exists in direct contradiction to a stated Guiding Principle of the Azalea Isles, the Plain Meaning Reading principle. "Can" is, as Plaintiff admits, a permissive word. Section 5(b), stating "Once this has been done the bill is proposed and they can notify the speaker to open debate." clearly shows an optional notification of the Speaker, when this legal principle is applied. "Can" is defined as "be able to" or "be permitted to" - the law, with this substituted definition, reads "...[the member] is able to / is permitted to notify the speaker to open debate." Nothing about that creates a "statutory handshake" or binding requirement on the Speaker.

(2) Plaintiff attempts to create a notification requirement by improper application of a reference to anti-surplusage canon, as outlined by the Court in Thegreatfired v. Lyon. However, Plaintiff ignores the clearer, more obvious statutory effect here. Section 5(a) establishes that any Member of Parliament may introduce legislation. Section 5(b) establishes that introduced bills are automatically considered proposed. Section 7(a), which states "Any bill that is introduced will be considered by the house as soon as the speaker opens the debate. Once the debate has opened members will be notified by the speaker." allows the Speaker to open debate.

7(a) does not say "Any bill that is introduced will be considered by the house when the member informs the Speaker to open debate." It says when the Speaker opens debate. It is the Speaker's explicit authority to push introduced legislation to debate. Interpreting the latter part of the clause, which contains optional phrasing, to have binding, mandatory effect under anti-surplusage ignores the existing, clear legal effect the clause already maintains. Interpreting the law in alignment with the Plaintiff's claims would be inserting language that was not added by the legislature and that is fundamentally unsupported by the facts, and the statute itself.

(3) Plaintiff draws on past cases of judicial review, such as Crumplesnatch v. Azalea and Crumplesnatch v. Bezzer, to make the case for judicial intervention. However, neither of these cases are proper precedent to apply to this case. The cases dealt with a violation of existing procedure, or a failure to follow procedures in the law. This case is about an interpretation of procedure - not, like Crumplesnatch v. Azalea, a question of whether a binding procedural document exists.

Plaintiff Anthony Org respectfully opposes both pending motions. The defense's reliance on PPA § 9(d) is misplaced: the statute says a member "may choose" to pursue an internal override motion, which is permissive rather than exclusive. I. The Motion to Dismiss Should Be Denied.

A. This Court has authority to interpret the PPA and adjudicate this dispute.

The defense frames this case as a purely internal parliamentary disagreement, but the filed complaint asks the Court to decide a classic judicial question: whether a public officer acted within the authority granted by statute and the Constitution. The Constitution provides that the Judiciary "shall interpret the law and uphold the constitution of Azalea Isles," and that the courts are responsible for "adjudicating disputes within Azalea Isles, through rendering verdicts in civil and criminal trials" (Const. art. 5). The Court separately recognizes declaratory judgments, writs of mandamus, and injunctions as ordinary judicial tools, including where a government official or entity must be compelled to perform or refrain from a duty required by law (Court Orders, Powers, and Judgments). A suit asking this Court to declare the meaning of PPA § 5(b), determine whether the Speaker exceeded that authority, and order lawful compliance therefore sits well within the Court's ordinary jurisdiction.

Azalean precedent confirms the point. In Crumplesnatch v. Azalea Isles (2025) CV 02, the Court expressly recognized Parliament's sovereign right to set its own procedures, but still reviewed a Speakership-related procedural dispute, concluded that the challenged vote was improper in light of the governing procedural framework, removed that vote from the tally, and ordered Parliament to clarify its procedure going forward. That is judicial review of parliamentary procedure in action. The case therefore defeats the defense's claim that separation of powers bars this Court from deciding whether the Speaker complied with the PPA.

B. PPA § 9(d) creates a permissive internal remedy, not an exclusive bar to suit.

The defense's principal theory is that Plaintiff should have used PPA § 9(d) instead of filing suit. But § 9(d) says only that if a member believes the Speaker has erred or acted partially, the member "may choose" to override the Speaker's decision through a parliamentary motion. PPA § 9(d). That language is permissive. It does not say the member must do so, that such a motion is the exclusive remedy, or that courts are divested of jurisdiction until such a motion is attempted. The defense cannot rely on a plain-meaning reading of "can" in § 5(b) while ignoring the equally plain permissive wording of "may choose" in § 9(d). If plain meaning matters, it cuts against exclusivity.

The legislative history of § 9(d) reinforces that reading. The Speakership Amendment Act explained that the override provision was adopted to "retain and protect the impartiality of the speaker" and to "limit the powers of the speaker by allowing parliament to override a decision made by the speaker" (Speakership Amendment Act § 2(2)-(3)). In other words, § 9(d) was enacted as an additional political check within Parliament, not as a jurisdiction-stripping provision that would prevent the Judiciary from performing its own constitutional duty to interpret the law. An internal override motion and a judicial declaration of legal meaning serve different functions. The former is political and contingent on securing support from other MPs; the latter answers what the law authorizes. Nothing in the PPA makes those remedies mutually exclusive.

C. The complaint alleges a direct injury and cognizable claims.

This is not an abstract disagreement about parliamentary philosophy. Plaintiff alleges that he introduced the Real Crime Crackdown Act, did not notify the Speaker to open debate, objected immediately when the Speaker claimed debate had opened, and was then told by the Speaker that he could open debate without Plaintiff's notice because he "need not notify" himself (Compl., Factual Allegations No. 3, 6-18). Plaintiff claims their reading upholds anti-surplusage canon and ensures every provision has an effect. Plaintiff again ignores the contradiction in the arguments they just delivered. If a provision (with permissive wording) creates an option, but not a requirement, as Plaintiff argued for Section 9(d) and the court upheld, then why now does a different provision with permissive wording create a binding requirement, rather than an option? Section 9(d), under this court’s interpretation, serves no real purpose if individual MPs can escape needing the support of two other MPs to reverse an action, simply by filing a lawsuit. Again - upholding Plaintiff’s interpretation means upholding an inherently flawed and contradictory position that undermines the legal foundation this court has already set in this case.

Plaintiff claims that the Speaker has acted partially, rejecting the publicly available evidence that another MP called one of the Speaker’s own bills into to debate and the Speaker allowed it. Any and all claims that the Speaker was acting partially fly in the face of the facts, and attempt to undermine the law’s clear position that the Speaker has the full rights and privileges of the other MPs, which include disliking a bill and stating so in debate. The Speaker acted in accordance with an interpretation that impacted his own legislation, and showed no bias. The only evidence the Plaintiff has provided to this court was the Speaker exercising his right to debate - there is nothing to show that opening debate early was a premeditated act of sabotage rather than an action consistent with the Speaker’s procedural interpretation

In all, Your Honor, as the court has seen fit to establish judicial review of Parliament procedures, the Defense has clearly outlined the problems with ruling in favor of the Plaintiff on this case. We look forward to continuing to demonstrate to Your Honor why a judgement should be returned in favor of the Defense.
