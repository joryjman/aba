As stated in The Guiding Principles of Azalea Isles and Article 5 of the Constitution of the Azalea Isles, only the Supreme Court is empowered to review constitutionality claims and subsequently overturn laws for violating the Constitution. Per article 5 "The Supreme Court also reviews the constitutionality of laws made by Parliament, removal of any position created by the Constitution, and deals with other cases not covered by other courts." and "The Supreme Court is responsible for interpreting the law while retaining the supremacy of the Constitution.". These sections are explicit that the Supreme Court is the one with the authority compared to the judiciary as a whole as other sections refer to "the courts" specifically and their general role. The District Court, as a lower court established by law, has no such power or authority to hear this case or rule on the constitutionality of a law. Whilst the Court Reformation Act states that the District Court will be the first to hear cases, this act alone does not have the authority to override the constitution. Therefore, your honour, you have no choice but to dismiss this case.

RULING ON MOTION TO DISMISS

The Court thanks the parties for their patience in awaiting this ruling.There were some technical difficulties, and given the importance of the issue, the Court wanted to ensure that the decision was properly researched and written.

The defence argues that the Guiding Principles of Azalea Isles and Article 5 of the Constitution of the Azalea Isles grant the Supreme Court exclusive jurisdiction to review constitutional claims and subsequently overturn laws for violating the Constitution.

The plaintiff argues that the Court Reformation Act grants the district courts authority to review constitutional claims and subsequently overturn laws for violating the Constitution. The plaintiff further argues that, if the Court were to decide that district courts cannot handle constitutional claims, then the district court could not overturn the Court Reformation Act either, thereby creating a contradiction.

Court Opinion:

The limited judicial precedent on this issue, found in the ongoing case of Aero Nox v. Azalea Isles, 2026 CV 22, points to the lack of an explicit exclusivity statement granting the Supreme Court exclusive original jurisdiction.

Furthermore, the text of the constitution also supports this interpretation. Although it is clear that a distinction is made between “courts” and the “Supreme Court,” this distinction is not applied consistently so as to give exclusive powers to the Supreme Court. Many responsibilities are ascribed to the Supreme Court, such as: “The Supreme Court is responsible for interpreting the law while retaining the supremacy of the Constitution.” that are core judicial powers, and is stated as such in Article 2: “The Judiciary shall interpret the law and uphold the Constitution of Azalea Isles.” It is not reasonable to assume that the Constitution intended to prevent parliament from allowing lower courts the ability to uphold the Constitution.

The Court finds that the phrase, “The Supreme Court also reviews the constitutionality of laws made by Parliament, removal of any position created by the Constitution, and deals with other cases not covered by other courts,” is intended to grant certain powers to the Supreme Court that cannot be taken away by statutory law, not necessarily to give the Supreme Court exclusive original jurisdiction. This interpretation is supported by the rest of the paragraph, which states that Parliament may create lower courts, but that the Supreme Court shall “retain” final appellate jurisdiction. This points to a clear intention to allow flexibility in statutory law, while retaining several core powers for the Supreme Court.

As for the Guiding Principles, they do not refer to the Supreme Court as having exclusive jurisdiction either. Moreover, they can be overridden by statutory law, meaning that the Court Reformation Act would supersede them regardless.

Therefore the motion to dismiss is denied.

Your Honor,

We intend to lay out exactly why Plaintiff's arguments are wrong.

First and foremost, Plaintiff continues to represent inspections as searches, because Plaintiff claims the sole purpose of an inspection is to gather evidence in order to levy criminal charges against industrial property owners. This is untrue. The primary purpose of the inspections is administrative, to ensure compliance with the law. The inspection is never entered into with a goal of finding a failure to follow the law. In fact, under the pre-amended version of the Industrial Regulation Act, if inspectors see any criminal activity that is unrelated to the Industrial Regulation Act, it is inadmissible. Only if they come across an IRA crime while doing the inspection is there any enforcement, and calling such enforcement the sole purpose of a normal regulatory inspection is a transparent misrepresentation.

This invalidates much of the rest of the Plaintiff's argument. These are not criminal searches; they are not done under suspicion of criminal activity. They are routine inspections. Beyond that, Plaintiff's reliance on the Guiding Principles is irrelevant, as "In places where statutes exist, the parliament law shall supersede the common law."

The Plaintiff then goes on to misapply Sagg Wizard v Ministry of Justice (2026) Cv18 to assert the Ministry of Urban Development cannot enforce penalties that arise from the inspection process specifically assigned to said Ministry. Sagg Wizard v Ministry of Justice clearly established that Parliament may empower Ministries to enforce criminal punishments through administrative procedure, via legislation. The Ministry of Urban Development is responsible for the inspection process and its enforcement; Plaintiff's arguments against that are the same narrow scrutiny arguments that the Supreme Court rejected in Aero Nox v. Azalea Isles (2025) CV 15's appeal.

Finally, the plaintiff claims unequal enforcement. However, there is a key difference between i006 and i008 that the Plaintiff has neglected to inform the court of; refusal of entry. As per the https://cityrp.org/threads/inspection-report-template.3355/, "The property can also fail its inspection if the inspector cannot obtain the information needed for the remaining inspection." There were additional areas of the plot where the inspector noticed a name tag but was denied entry to. Such denial, under the law, is treated as an automatic failure, not even counting the inspector being unable to obtain the necessary information due to the restrictions, and did not happen with i006. The two inspections are distinct because of this. They cannot be treated as the same case, and as such do not violate equal treatment. It is because of the Plaintiff's denial that the inspector was unable to properly verify the lack of machinery on the property, and the assertion there is no machinery is only word of mouth, not a passed inspection. It was the deliberate refusal that caused the Plaintiff's inspection failure, in keeping with the law and published template.
