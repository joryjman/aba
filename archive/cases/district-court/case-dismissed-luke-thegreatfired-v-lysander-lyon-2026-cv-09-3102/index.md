Motion to Dismiss

Your Honor, there are several important issues here that warrant dismissal.

(1) Your Honor set a clear precedent in Jebediah Crumplesnatch v The Prime Minister. The Plaintiff in this case is requesting both government action (Relief request #2) and personal compensation (Relief requests #1 and #3). Not only does the Defendant have no way to grant the second relief request, as he is no longer a member of the Government, these joint requests raise the same issue Your Honor dismissed Jebediah Crumplesnatch v The Prime Minister for. If the Plaintiff has a claim against the Government, he must raise it with the Government, not with the Defendant. Moreover, as outlined in precedent, the Plaintiff must outline how the Defendant’s actions fall outside the scope of official duties, if the Defendant is to be held personally and individually responsible.

(2) The Plaintiff is not the owner of the alleged copyright. Parliament is the owner of the copyright, as outlined in Section 4(a)iv of the Establish Copyright Protections Act. If the Plaintiff wishes to dispute how Parliament has managed the alleged copyright, that is not an issue that involves the Defendant. As the Plaintiff is not the owner of the copyright, the Plaintiff does not have the standing to sue an individual for any alleged infringement of copyright - only Parliament. Your Honor previously rejected this exact type of secondhand legal claim on behalf of another party in Vontobel v Ministry of Urban Development.

(3) The Plaintiff’s Establish Copyright Protections Act is also quite clear, in Section 6a, that cases involving Parliament-owned copyright cannot be brought to court. “All cases of copyright infringement shall be processed and filed through the courts as civil lawsuits unless copyright is held by the Parliament of Azalea.” The Plain Meaning Reading principle makes this clause, which has no other clarifying provisions, clear. Even if Your Honor would allow the Plaintiff to request government relief from a private citizen, in violation of Jebediah Crumplesnatch v The Prime Minister, and even if Your Honor would allow a secondhand legal claim from an individual who does not own the copyright, in violation of Vontobel v Ministry of Urban Development, the law here is unambiguous. There cannot be a civil lawsuit involving Parliament-owned copyright.

While there are other factors the Defendant could raise, in the interest of judicial efficiency, I will avoid doing so. Fundamentally, this is a lawsuit that flies in the face of precedent established by two Supreme Court rulings; requests relief the Defendant cannot provide; and is explicitly disallowed by the Plaintiff’s own legislation.

As such, the Defendant moves for an immediate dismissal, with prejudice.

Thank you both for your arguments. After some consideration, the Court has decided to accept the motion to dismiss due to lack of standing. While I understand the Plaintiff has formulated a detailed case here, unfortunately the Establish Copyright Protections Act is unambiguous regarding this issue. It is noted in §4(a)(iv) that "all published bills and otherwise works published or done in association with the Parliament of Azalea, will automatically be transferred into the ownership of the parliament," affirming that ownership of the bill is held by the Parliament, not by the Plaintiff.

The requirement that copyright be "officially registered" applies to party-driven transfers (such as purchase, succession, or renunciation) where registration serves to evidence and track conveyance. By contrast, §4(a)(iv) operates differently: it mandates automatic vesting of ownership in Parliament by operation of law upon publication of a bill in association with Parliament. Requiring registration for such vesting would impose procedural obligations nowhere specified in the Act and contradict the statute's use of mandatory language ("will automatically be transferred"). The Court therefore construes the registration requirement as inapplicable to statutory vesting under §4(a)(iv).

Considering the principle of the anti-surplusage canon, statutes must be interpreted so that every provision has effect. Under the Plaintiff's logic, the entire purpose of §4(a)(iv) would be nullified and untriggerable if it relied solely on voluntary registration through the Ministry of Economic Affairs. Therefore, while this logic may be clever, it is legally incorrect and does not hold water in the District Court. It can be clearly seen through the wording of this law and legislative intent that Parliament retains ownership over published bills. The statute's text explicitly says it is automatic, and therefore it is an automatic vestiture, not a voluntary choice.

Given that Parliament retains ownership over bills, §6(a) stipulates that "(a) All cases of copyright infringement shall be processed and filed through the courts as civil lawsuits unless copyright is held by the Parliament of Azalea. A copyright does not need to be officially registered in order for copyright infringement to occur but is greatly encouraged for it to be." This specific exemption to "unless copyright is held by the Parliament is Azalea" prevents the Plaintiff from having any sort of standing in this case.

The Court thanks both parties for their time. This case is hereby dismissed.

The Court will be respectfully rejecting the motion to reconsider, as it presents no newly discovered evidence, intervening change in law, or clear error of law.

The Court has already addressed and rejected the Plaintiff's proposed interpretation of §4(a)(iv), and the motion merely restates arguments previously considered. The statute's automatic vesting of ownership in Parliament operates by law upon publication and is not conditioned on voluntary registration. Further, the absence of a private civil remedy where Parliament holds copyright does not constitute unconstitutional overreach. As the Plaintiff lacks standing as a matter of law, this case cannot proceed.

The Establish Copyright Act's provisions are unambiguous regarding this matter. It clearly states that all published bills "will automatically be transferred into the ownership of the parliament" and prohibits civil lawsuits for copyright held by the Parliament of Azalea Isles. While I sympathize with the Plaintiff in the fact that their work may not have been credited, this is common with legislation, with many bills enabling a repeal/replace mechanism and or using similar language as other bills to ensure consistency. It is beyond clear why this automatic vestiture clause was created in the statute.

If the Plaintiff is alleging that the statutory mechanisms itself (the Establish Copyright Act) is unconstitutional due to government overreach, this case is not being levied against the government. It is being levied against an individual. As held in Crumplesnatch v. MilkCrack (2025) CV 03, an individual cannot be held personally liable for official acts within their government duties. If the Plaintiff is alleging that the Defendant, Lysander Lyon, violated his constitutional rights by proposing a bill as an MP; his legal grievance is with the government (the Parliament of Azalea Isles), as proposing a bill is within the Defendant's official duties as an MP.

Accordingly, the Court's previous decision stands. I would like to once again thank both parties for their time.
